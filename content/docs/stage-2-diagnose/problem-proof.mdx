This module answers a brutal question: is the problem real enough to build
around? A problem can sound smart, but if it does not cost people time,
money, or risk, it will not survive.

You will score the pain with evidence, not enthusiasm. You will prove which
problem is worth your next test.

**Output for this module**
- Problem scorecard for top 3 candidates
- Evidence notes for each score
- One prioritized problem to take into Module 3

## What "Pain Worth Solving" Means
Pain worth solving is not a preference. It is a repeated cost in time,
money, or risk.

It usually shows up like this:
- It happens often enough to be remembered.
- It creates a visible cost (time loss, money loss, risk).
- People already try to fix it.
- Workarounds are active and repeatable.

If those signals are missing, the idea is likely noise.

## The Problem Scorecard
Use four factors to score each problem from 1 to 5:

1. **Frequency**: How often does the pain show up?
2. **Intensity**: How costly is it when it hits?
3. **Spend**: Is there evidence of time or money spent?
4. **Workarounds**: Are people actively patching it today?

Diagram cue: a simple scorecard grid with four rows and a total.

## A Real Example (Raden vs Away)
Raden built smart luggage with batteries, tracking, and tech upgrades. It
sold out early, then shut down in 2018. The features were clever, but the
pain was not deep enough to sustain the business. In the same market, Away
scaled by focusing on the core pain of travel: reliable luggage, better
experience, and customer service.

Nice-to-have collapsed. Painful alternative took off. That is the lesson.

## How to Score Each Factor
These are not perfect numbers. They are consistent signals.

**1) Frequency**
- 1 = rare (a few times a year)
- 3 = monthly
- 5 = weekly or daily

**2) Intensity**
- 1 = mild annoyance
- 3 = real friction, but tolerable
- 5 = real cost, risk, or lost revenue

**3) Spend**
- 1 = no spend, no time invested
- 3 = time investment or small tools
- 5 = clear spend or paid solutions

**4) Workarounds**
- 1 = nothing in place
- 3 = light hacks or manual steps
- 5 = repeatable, visible workarounds

If you are not sure, score lower. Overconfidence hides weak pain.

## Types of Pain (Use the Right Lens)
Not every pain looks the same. Use the right lens when you score.

**Time pain**
- Repeated delays
- Manual steps that waste hours
- Coordination overhead

**Money pain**
- Direct spend on tools or services
- Lost revenue from delays or churn
- Refunds, penalties, or write-offs

**Risk pain**
- Compliance exposure
- Mistakes that trigger rework
- Missed deadlines with real consequences

If you cannot name the pain type, the problem is not clear yet.

## Common Workaround Patterns
Workarounds are evidence. Look for:
- Spreadsheets used as systems
- Slack threads replacing process
- Manual checklists or templates
- People paying for freelancers or agencies
- Duplicate tools doing the same job

## Evidence You Should Use
Scores without evidence are just guesses. Use:
- Interview notes that include actions and costs
- Screenshots of workarounds (spreadsheets, checklists)
- Examples of paid tools or services
- Proof of time loss (hours, delays, rework)

No evidence = no points.

**Evidence strength scale (quick)**
- 1: Opinion or hope
- 2: Single anecdote
- 3: Multiple interviews with repeatable behavior
- 4: Visible workaround or spend in several cases
- 5: Paid commitment or proof you can verify

## Evidence Prompts (By Factor)
Use these questions to collect evidence for each score.

**Frequency**
- "When was the last time this happened?"
- "How often does it show up in a normal week?"

**Intensity**
- "What breaks when it happens?"
- "What does it cost you when it goes wrong?"

**Spend**
- "Have you paid for anything to solve it?"
- "What tools do you already use for this?"

**Workarounds**
- "What do you do instead today?"
- "Can you show me how you handle it?"

## Score Interpretation
Use the score to decide what happens next.

- **16 to 20:** Strong candidate. Move forward.
- **12 to 15:** Possible candidate. Tighten evidence.
- **Below 12:** Weak candidate. Replace or reframe.

Do not treat the score as a verdict. Treat it as a filter.

## Keep the Lens Consistent
Only compare problems within the same ICP. A pain that is severe for a
small business may look mild inside an enterprise and vice versa.

If your evidence comes from different personas, split the scores. Do not
average across unrelated groups. That creates false confidence.

Consistency beats precision here.

## What a Strong Score Looks Like
A strong problem usually hits:
- 16 to 20 total score
- Evidence across at least two sources
- Clear spend or workaround

If your top score is under 12, do not force it. Go back and find a better
problem. This is cheaper now than later.

## Strong vs Weak (Generic)
**Strong**
- "Weekly delays in handoffs cause missed client deadlines."
- "Teams pay for two tools and still use spreadsheets."

**Weak**
- "It would be nice if this looked cleaner."
- "It is annoying but we live with it."

## Symptom vs Root Pain (Quick Check)
Symptoms are visible. Root pain is the cause.

Example:
- Symptom: "I forget to post content."
- Root pain: "I do not have a reliable planning system and I lose momentum."

Symptoms get polite interest. Root pain gets commitment.

## The Root Cause Drill (Short)
If you are unsure, ask "why" two or three times:
1. Why does this happen?
2. Why does that matter?
3. Why has it not been fixed?

This helps you see whether you are solving the symptom or the real problem.

## Quantify the Cost (Lightly)
You do not need a spreadsheet model. You need a rough cost.

Ask:
- How many hours are lost each week?
- Who is affected?
- What does that cost in time or revenue?

Even a rough estimate helps you compare two problems that feel equal.

## The Cost Ladder (Quick Estimate)
Estimate the cost with the simplest math possible.

- Hours lost per week x hourly cost = time cost
- Lost deals x average deal size = revenue cost
- Errors x cleanup time = rework cost

You are not looking for perfect numbers. You are looking for a clear
direction.

## Evidence Artifact
Your evidence artifact is a scored shortlist that includes:
- Problem statement
- Score total
- Evidence notes
- Why this problem outranks the others

This becomes your anchor for Stage 2, Module 3.

**One-page problem proof summary**
- Problem statement (one sentence)
- Total score (with factor scores)
- 2 to 3 evidence bullets
- Cost estimate (rough)
- Current workaround

## Problem Proof Checklist
Before you move on, confirm:
- I can name the last time the pain showed up.
- I have at least one concrete workaround example.
- I can explain the cost in one sentence.
- I have a score and evidence notes to back it up.

If any of these are missing, keep working the evidence.

## Mini Proof Test (Optional)
If you want one more signal, ask for a tiny commitment:
- A paid pilot
- A deposit
- A calendar booking with a clear outcome

You do not need a full offer yet. You need proof that the pain creates
action, not just agreement.

## Tie-Breaker Rules
If two problems score similarly, choose the one with:
- More visible spend
- Clearer workarounds
- Faster access to buyers

Speed to evidence beats perfect certainty here.

## What to Ignore for Now
Do not chase:
- Feature ideas
- Long-term expansion markets
- Edge-case requests

You are proving pain, not designing the final product.

## If Evidence Is Mixed
Mixed evidence is normal. Do not force a conclusion.

If half your interviews show real pain and half do not:
- Tighten the ICP
- Run 2 to 3 more interviews
- Re-score with the new evidence

Clarity often comes after one more round.

## If Spend Is Missing
Sometimes the pain is real but spend is zero. That usually means one of
two things:
1) The buyer has no budget for it yet, or
2) The problem is not painful enough to justify spend.

If spend is missing, look for time cost or risk. If those are also weak,
drop the problem. If time or risk is strong, keep testing and focus on the
buyer who actually owns the budget.

## Common Traps
- Falling for enthusiasm without spend.
- Scoring based on your gut, not evidence.
- Ignoring workarounds because they look informal.
- Choosing a problem that is "interesting" instead of costly.

Enthusiasm is cheap. Evidence is expensive. You need expensive.

## Reality Check (30 Seconds)
Ask yourself:
- "Would someone pay to make this disappear?"
- "Can I point to a workaround that already exists?"
- "Does this pain show up often enough to matter?"

If the answer is no, the problem is not ready.

If you can explain the pain to a stranger in one sentence and it still
sounds expensive, you are close. If you need five minutes and a slide deck,
you are not. Clarity is the first proof.

## When You Can Move On
You are ready for Module 3 when:
- One problem clearly outranks the rest
- You can point to repeatable evidence
- You can explain the cost in plain language

If those are not true, keep proving the pain.

## The Bridge to Module 3
Once you have a high-score problem, you must ask one more question:
what do people already use instead?

**Closing line**
If the score is low, the idea is noise.
