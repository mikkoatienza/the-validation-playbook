A strong problem is not enough. You also need a clear wedge. If people are
already solving the problem in a way that feels "good enough," you are late.

A wedge is a narrow, defensible angle that wins a specific segment.

This module maps what people already use: competitors, substitutes, and
DIY workarounds. You will surface the gap and decide whether you can win it.

**Output for this module**
- Alternatives grid (direct, adjacent, DIY)
- Gap statement + underserved segment note
- Clear differentiation angle or a decision to drop the idea

## Competitor vs Substitute (Plain Language)
A competitor is what people buy instead of you in the same category.
A substitute is what people use to solve the problem without your category.

Example:
- Competitor: another scheduling app
- Substitute: spreadsheets, email, or a shared calendar

If you only scan competitors, you miss the real battlefield.

## The Three Buckets
You will map alternatives in three buckets:

1. **Direct competitors**: same category, same promise
2. **Adjacent substitutes**: different category, same outcome
3. **DIY workarounds**: manual systems people build themselves

DIY is where most early-stage founders lose. The customer does not need a
product if their spreadsheet already works.

## Why DIY Persists
DIY workarounds survive because they are already paid for: time.
People tolerate them until the pain outweighs the hassle.

If your alternative is a spreadsheet, your job is not to compete on
features. Your job is to make the cost of DIY obvious and the switch
feel safe.

Ask yourself:
- What does DIY steal every week?
- What breaks when DIY fails?
- Who pays the hidden cost?

If you cannot answer those, you cannot beat the DIY substitute.

## Direct vs Substitute (Quick Test)
Ask yourself:
- "If my product vanished, what would people do tomorrow?"
- "What did they use before this category existed?"

The answers are your real substitutes.

## What You Are Looking For
Not a list of logos. A list of habits.

Find out:
- What people already pay for
- What they are willing to tolerate
- Where they still complain
- What they wish they did not have to do

That is where the gap lives.

Diagram cue: a simple 3-column alternatives grid.

## Exercise: Build the Alternatives Grid
Make the grid for your top problem.

**Alternatives grid**
| Direct | Adjacent | DIY |
| --- | --- | --- |
| | | |

Under each, add:
- Price (if known)
- Promise (what outcome it sells)
- Weak spot (what people still hate)

The weak spot is your entry point.

## Where to Look (Fast Sources)
Use places where people already complain or compare:

- Review sites (G2, Capterra, App Store)
- Forum threads ("best alternative to...")
- Reddit, Slack groups, niche communities
- "Switching to X from Y" blog posts
- Job listings (tools they already use)

If you cannot find any alternatives, you probably do not understand the
problem yet.

## 60-Minute Alternatives Sprint
If you want a fast scan, use this:

- 15 minutes: list direct competitors (from Google and review sites)
- 15 minutes: list adjacent substitutes (other categories)
- 15 minutes: list DIY workarounds (spreadsheets, templates, manual process)
- 15 minutes: capture weak spots and pricing

Speed beats perfection here. You can refine later.

## Evidence Artifact
Your evidence artifact is a one-page scan that includes:
- The alternatives grid
- The gap statement
- The underserved segment you can own
- One piece of evidence that the gap is real

This is your proof that the space is not already owned.

## Evidence Notes (Simple)
For each alternative, capture:
- Who uses it
- Why they chose it
- What they still dislike
- What it costs them (money or time)

Without evidence, your gap is just a guess.

## Language Clues
The words people use tell you where the gap is.
Capture phrases like:
- "I hate that I still have to..."
- "It works, but..."
- "We made our own because..."

These phrases usually point to a missing workflow you can own.

## Alternatives Snapshot (One Page)
Before you finish the scan, summarize in one page:
- Top 3 direct competitors + why people pick them
- Top 3 substitutes + why they "work"
- Top 3 DIY workarounds + why they persist

This snapshot becomes your reference when you craft the offer later.

## Gap Statement (Template)
Use this to define your wedge:

"For [ICP], the current options [do X], but [pain remains]. We will win by
[approach] for [underserved segment]."

Example (generic):
"For small clinics, scheduling tools handle appointments, but no-shows still
kill revenue. We will win by reducing no-shows through deposit-first flows
for clinics with under 20 staff."

If you cannot write this statement, you do not own a gap.

## Segment Notes (One Paragraph)
Write a short paragraph describing the segment you can own:
- Who they are
- What they do weekly
- Why the gap hurts them most

If you cannot do this in five sentences, the segment is too wide.

## Proof Examples (What Counts)
Examples of evidence that the gap is real:
- Three reviews that complain about the same missing workflow
- Two interviews where the buyer names the same workaround
- A thread where people ask for an alternative that does not exist

## Gap Validation Questions
Before you commit, answer:
- Can I name a segment that feels this gap most?
- Can I reach them this month?
- Will they pay to close the gap?

If any answer is unclear, the gap is still a hypothesis.

## Segment Size vs Access
A small segment you can reach beats a large segment you cannot.

Ask:
- Do I know where they gather?
- Can I get 5 interviews this week?
- Can I describe a clear trigger that makes them look for a solution?

If access is weak, your wedge is theoretical. Fix access before you build.

## Pricing Reality Check
If your alternative is free or already budgeted, you are not competing on
features. You are competing on switching cost.

Ask:
- "What would make someone pay for this now?"
- "What cost is bigger than the switching cost?"

## Switching Cost Checklist
Switching is not just money. It is risk, time, and trust.

Check:
- How long does setup take?
- What data must be moved?
- Who is afraid of disruption?
- What happens if it fails?

If switching cost is high, your wedge must be stronger.

## Alternative Weak Spots (Look For These)
When people complain, listen for:
- Slow setup or long onboarding
- Hidden costs or pricing surprises
- Lack of a key workflow
- Poor support or reliability issues

These are often better wedges than "better features."

## Alternative Score (Quick)
If you are unsure which alternatives matter most, score each one:

- **Adoption**: how many people use it now?
- **Satisfaction**: do they like it or tolerate it?
- **Switching cost**: is it easy to leave?

The best wedge often sits where adoption is high but satisfaction is low.

## Competitive Teardown Questions
Use these to understand why alternatives win:
- What promise do they make on the homepage?
- What is the first action they ask users to take?
- What do reviews praise or complain about most?

If you can answer these for the top three alternatives, you understand
the battlefield.

## Wedge Options (Simple Types)
If you struggle to name the wedge, try one of these:
- **Speed wedge:** faster to value than existing tools
- **Access wedge:** serves an ignored segment
- **Outcome wedge:** guarantees a specific result
- **Workflow wedge:** fits how work actually happens

Pick one. Multiple wedges early usually means none.

## Wedge Test (Quick)
Pressure-test your wedge:
- "Could a big competitor copy this in a week?"
- "Would a buyer care enough to switch?"
- "Is this a real constraint or just a preference?"

If the wedge fails here, it will fail in the market.

## Positioning One-Liner
Write one sentence that contrasts you with the dominant alternative.

Template:
"Unlike [alternative], we help [ICP] achieve [outcome] by [wedge]."

Example (generic):
"Unlike generic task tools, we help clinic admins reduce no-shows by
automating deposits and reminders for small clinics."

If you cannot write this line without hedging, your wedge is still weak.

## Common Traps
- Overestimating differentiation because your product feels newer.
- Ignoring DIY because it looks informal.
- Assuming brand loyalty is a moat in early markets.
- Thinking "no competition" is good. It usually means no demand.

If you cannot name competitors, you are not early. You are unclear.

## Reality Check (30 Seconds)
Ask yourself:
- "Is my gap real or just marketing?"
- "Would a buyer switch if this disappeared?"
- "Do I have evidence beyond my own belief?"

If the answer is no, tighten the scan.

## What to Ignore for Now
Do not chase:
- Feature comparisons you cannot win
- Legacy competitors with locked-in contracts
- Edge-case substitutes that do not touch your ICP

Stay focused on the alternatives your buyer mentions.

## A Real Example (Blockbuster vs Netflix)
Blockbuster dominated video rentals. Netflix started with mail-order DVDs,
then moved to streaming. Blockbuster saw the shift but did not move fast
enough to match the substitute. By 2010, Blockbuster filed for bankruptcy.

Their real competitor was not just other rental stores. It was a new way to
get movies without leaving home. That substitute killed them.

That is why you scan substitutes, not just competitors.

## Final Sanity Check
If a competitor already solves the problem well and cheaply, your best move
is not to outbuild them. It is to find the ignored segment they overlook.

If you cannot find that segment, drop the idea. This is a win.

## When You Can Move On
You are done with this module when:
- You can name three direct competitors and three substitutes
- You have a clear gap statement
- You have evidence that your segment is underserved

If you cannot do that yet, keep scanning. This saves you from building a
copycat.

**Closing line**
If you cannot name the gap, you do not own a wedge.
