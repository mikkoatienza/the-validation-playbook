A strong problem is not enough. You also need a clear wedge. If people are already solving the problem in a way that feels "good enough," you are late. A wedge is a narrow, defensible angle that wins a specific segment.

<Callout type="idea" title="Output for this module">
- Alternatives grid (direct, adjacent, DIY)
- Gap statement + underserved segment note
- Clear differentiation angle or a decision to drop the idea
</Callout>

<Callout type="warn" title="DIY is your real competition">
DIY workarounds survive because they are already paid for: time. The customer does not need a product if their spreadsheet already works. Your job is to make the cost of DIY obvious and the switch feel safe. Blockbuster's real competitor was not other rental stores -- it was a new way to get movies without leaving home.
</Callout>

## Gap Statement

<div className="mt-4 rounded-xl border-2 border-fd-border p-5" style={{backgroundColor: 'hsl(var(--fd-muted) / 0.4)'}}>
  <p className="text-[10px] font-semibold uppercase tracking-wider" style={{color: 'hsl(var(--stage-2))'}}>Gap Statement Formula</p>
  <p className="mt-3 font-[family-name:var(--font-display)] text-xl text-fd-foreground">"For <strong>[ICP]</strong>, the current options <strong>[do X]</strong>, but <strong>[pain remains]</strong>. We will win by <strong>[approach]</strong> for <strong>[underserved segment]</strong>."</p>
  <div className="mt-4 rounded-lg border-2 p-4" style={{borderColor: 'hsl(var(--stage-2) / 0.4)', backgroundColor: 'hsl(var(--stage-2) / 0.06)'}}>

Pick one. Multiple wedges early usually means none.

<Callout type="error" title="Common traps">
- Overestimating differentiation because your product feels newer
- Ignoring DIY because it looks informal
- Assuming brand loyalty is a moat in early markets
- Thinking "no competition" is good -- it usually means no demand
</Callout>
