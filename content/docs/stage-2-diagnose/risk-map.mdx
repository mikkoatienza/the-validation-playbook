Founders do not usually fail because they missed a feature. They fail because
they built on a belief that was never tested.

This module helps you surface that belief before it eats months of time.
You will map your assumption stack, identify the riskiest one, and choose
the first test you must run.

**Output for this module**
- 8 to 12 "we believe" statements
- A ranked risk map (importance vs evidence)
- The single assumption you must test next

## The Assumption Stack
An assumption is a belief about the world that must be true for your idea
to work. When you stack ten assumptions together, your idea becomes a
tower. The question is which block, if pulled, collapses the whole thing.

Most ideas rely on the same layers:
1. **Problem**: The pain is real and frequent.
2. **Person**: The right buyer cares and can pay.
3. **Willingness**: They will commit time or money.
4. **Reach**: You can find them reliably.
5. **Delivery**: You can solve it at a reasonable cost.

If any layer is wrong, the idea fails. That is why you test the weakest
layer first.

## The Risk Map
The Risk Map is a simple 2x2:
- **Importance**: If this is wrong, does the idea die?
- **Evidence**: How much proof do you have right now?

The danger zone is **high importance + low evidence**. That is your first
test.

**Example (generic)**
- Assumption: "HR managers will pay $49/month for onboarding checklists."
  - Importance: 5
  - Evidence: 1
- Assumption: "They already use spreadsheets for onboarding."
  - Importance: 3
  - Evidence: 4
- Assumption: "We can reach them through LinkedIn messages."
  - Importance: 4
  - Evidence: 2

The top risk here is the first one: price willingness with low evidence.

Importance matters because it tells you what can kill the idea. Evidence
matters because it tells you what you already know. Your job is to find
the gap between the two and close it fast.

## A Real Example (Webvan)
Webvan built massive delivery infrastructure on the assumption that enough
people would switch to online grocery at scale. Demand did not match the
cost structure, and the company shut down in 2001. The hidden assumption
was not just "people want groceries delivered." It was "enough people will
change habits fast enough to make this model work."

Wrong assumption, wrong order, real collapse.

## The Core Rule
One wrong assumption can erase months of work. Test the riskiest one first.

If you test the easy assumption, you only feel progress. If you test the
riskiest one, you get truth.

Most founders avoid the riskiest test because it threatens the dream. That
is exactly why you should run it. Evidence does not care how attached you
are. It only cares if the assumption survives contact with reality.

## Common Assumption Types
Use these to force specificity:
- **Frequency**: "This happens weekly, not yearly."
- **Buyer**: "The manager can approve $200 without procurement."
- **Price**: "They will pay $99/month for the outcome."
- **Reach**: "We can get 20 qualified interviews in 2 weeks."
- **Switching**: "They can change tools without risk or disruption."
- **Outcome**: "This will reduce time by at least 30%."

If you cannot write an assumption in one sentence, it is too vague to test.

## Assumption Writing Rules
- One assumption per line.
- No "and" or "or" statements.
- Use a number when possible (price, time, frequency).

Bad: "They will pay and refer their friends."  
Better: "They will pay $99/month within 30 days."

## Assumption Stack Example (Generic)
Idea: "A tool that helps small clinics reduce no-shows."

Assumption stack:
- Clinics have no-show rates above 10%.
- The office manager feels the pain weekly.
- They already use workarounds (calls, reminders, deposits).
- They can approve $150/month without procurement.
- We can reach them through local clinic groups.
- We can reduce no-shows by at least 25%.

You do not need to be right yet. You need to be explicit.

## What Counts as Evidence
Evidence is behavior you can point to, not hope.

Stronger evidence:
- Past spend on similar tools
- Repeated workarounds in real workflows
- Clear time or money cost tied to the pain

Weaker evidence:
- "That sounds cool"
- "I would use it"
- Likes, follows, and polite praise

If your evidence is weak, mark it as weak. Honesty beats confidence here.

**Evidence sources (practical)**
- Interview notes with concrete examples
- Screenshots of workarounds (spreadsheets, templates)
- Payment proof or budget line references
- Signed LOIs or paid pilots

## Exercise: Build Your Assumption List
Write 8 to 12 "we believe" statements for your top ICP-problem pair.

Use these prompts:
- We believe the pain shows up at least [frequency].
- We believe the buyer can approve [price] without delays.
- We believe they already pay for [tool/workaround].
- We believe we can reach them through [channel].
- We believe switching does not create major risk.

Do not make them vague. If it cannot be tested, it does not count.

**Assumption dump template**
- We believe [role] feels [pain] [frequency].
- We believe they will pay [price] for [outcome].
- We believe they currently use [workaround].
- We believe we can reach them via [channel].
- We believe switching costs are [low/high] because [reason].

## Exercise: Score Importance and Evidence
For each assumption, score:
- **Importance (1-5):** If wrong, does the idea break?
- **Evidence (1-5):** How much proof do you already have?

Guidance:
- Importance 5 = the idea dies if wrong
- Evidence 1 = only a guess
- Evidence 5 = observed behavior or real spend

If you are unsure, score lower. Overconfidence hides risk.

**Evidence scoring guide (quick)**
- 1: Pure opinion or founder belief
- 2: A few anecdotes, no proof
- 3: Multiple interviews with repeatable behavior
- 4: Clear workaround or spend in multiple cases
- 5: Paid commitment or behavior you can verify

## Define a Pass Line
Before you test the top risk, define what "pass" looks like.

Examples:
- "3 buyers agree to a paid pilot."
- "5 people show a real workaround in live interviews."
- "2 teams approve a paid trial."

No pass line, no decision. A test without a pass line is just activity.

## Fast Test Formats (Pick One)
You do not need a full product to test a risk. Choose the smallest test
that produces real behavior.

Common options:
- **Interview proof:** get 5 real stories with spend/workarounds.
- **Paid pilot:** ask for a small paid trial or LOI.
- **Landing page:** run a waitlist with a clear action.
- **Concierge test:** deliver the outcome manually.

The goal is not a perfect experiment. The goal is a fast signal.

## Test Card (One Assumption)
Write this before you run any test:
- We believe [assumption].
- To test this, we will [smallest action].
- We will measure [signal].
- We are right if [pass line].
- Next step if right: [move forward].
- Next step if wrong: [change/stop].

One card per test keeps you focused.

## Timebox the Test
Give yourself a short window so you do not drift.

Simple rule:
- 7 to 10 days for the first test
- If no signal, change the assumption or the test

Speed keeps you honest. Long tests hide weak ideas.

## Circle the Top Three Risks
Pick the three assumptions with the highest importance and lowest evidence.

Then choose the #1 risk you will test first. That is your critical path.

If you are stuck, pick the assumption that would make the rest irrelevant
if it is false. That is usually the one to test first.

## What to Ignore for Now
Low-importance assumptions can wait. Do not spend energy proving things
that do not decide the outcome.

Ignore for now:
- Nice-to-have features
- Long-term expansion ideas
- Secondary channels you will not use this month

The goal is not to answer everything. The goal is to remove the single
assumption that could kill the idea.

## Evidence Artifact
Capture this as a simple list:
- Assumption
- Importance score
- Evidence score
- Why it matters

This is your map for Stage 2. It keeps you from testing the wrong thing.

## Pre-Mortem (2 Minutes)
Imagine your idea failed six months from now. Why?

Write three reasons. Then convert each into an assumption you can test.
This simple step reveals blind spots you would otherwise protect.

## Assumption Sanity Check
Before you move on, ask:
- Is this assumption about behavior, not opinions?
- Is it testable within two weeks?
- Would a "fail" actually change my decision?

If the answer is no, rewrite the assumption.

## Weekly Risk Review
Keep the map alive. Every week, ask:
- Which assumption now has evidence?
- Which new risk appeared after our last test?
- What is the next most dangerous assumption?

This turns risk mapping into a habit, not a one-time exercise.

## If Evidence Is Mixed
Mixed evidence happens. Do not force a decision.

If two interviews show pain and three do not, tighten the ICP and test
again. If the numbers stay split, your problem or buyer is still unclear.

## How Many Signals Are Enough?
You do not need dozens of data points. You need a repeatable pattern.

As a rough guide:
- 3 to 5 consistent examples can justify moving forward.
- 0 to 1 examples usually means you should change the assumption.
- Mixed signals mean refine the audience and test again.

Do not chase perfect certainty. Chase clear evidence.

## When You Can Move On
You are ready for Module 2 when:
- You have one top risk and a clear pass line.
- You know exactly what evidence would change your decision.

If you cannot name those, you are not done yet.

## Final Check (30 Seconds)
Ask yourself:
- "If this assumption fails, would I still build?"
- "Do I know how to test it in the next two weeks?"

If the answer is no, pause. The map is telling you where to focus.

## Common Traps
- Testing what is easy instead of what is deadly.
- Confusing opinions with evidence.
- Writing assumptions so broad they cannot be tested.
- Mixing five assumptions into one test.

One test. One risk. One clear pass line.

## The Handoff to Module 2
Now you will prove the problem is frequent and costly using the scorecard.
You are not validating the whole business. You are validating the pain.

**Closing line**
If the top risk is wrong, the idea is dead.
