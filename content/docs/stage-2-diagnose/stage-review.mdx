title: "Stage 2 Review"
description: "Checklist, readiness gate, and retrospective for Stage 2: Diagnose."
---

You entered this stage with a list. You leave with a decision. That is the
point. Stage 2 is where you stop treating every problem as equal and choose
the one that earns the build.

## The Three Artifacts That Matter
If you completed Stage 2 as designed, you now have:

- A Risk Map with the top assumption ranked
- A Problem Scorecard backed by evidence
- An Alternatives Grid with a clear gap or segment

That is the minimum foundation for Stage 3.

## Readiness Gate (30 Seconds)
Use this compact gate to decide if you should move on:

- **Risk Map:** top risk is named and testable this month
- **Problem Scorecard:** strong score with evidence, not opinions
- **Alternatives Grid:** clear gap or underserved segment
- **Wedge Statement:** “We win because [gap] for [segment].”

If any box is empty, slow down and fill it.

## If the Evidence Is Thin
Avoid forcing the next stage. Use one of these fixes first:

- Rewrite the top assumption and retest it
- Narrow the problem and rescore it
- Scan substitutes again with fresh eyes
- Drop the weakest segment and focus on the strongest

The goal is not to be clever. The goal is to be right.

## Module Recap (What Each One Gave You)
Stage 2 worked because each module did a specific job:

- **Risk Map** exposed the assumption that could kill the idea.
- **Problem Proof** showed whether the pain is frequent and costly.
- **Alternatives Scan** revealed if the space is already owned.

If any module feels weak, fix that weakness before you move on. A weak
diagnosis leads to a weak offer.

## Stage 2 Retrospective (Two Questions)
Write short answers before you move forward:

- Which assumption surprised you the most?
- Which alternative changed your view of the space?

That reflection will make your offer sharper in Stage 3.

## Decision Summary (One Sentence)
Write this line and keep it with your artifacts:

“We will build for [buyer] because [problem] scores high and the gap is
[evidence].”

If you cannot write that line, the diagnosis is not finished.

## Evidence Snapshot (One Paragraph)
Before you move on, write a single paragraph that captures:

- The top risk and how you tested it
- The strongest piece of pain evidence
- The gap you believe you can win

Keep it tight. If you cannot write it in one paragraph, the evidence is still
too fuzzy. Tighten it now while the research is fresh.

## Carry‑Forward Signals
Before you enter Stage 3, underline the strongest proof in your notes:

- The riskiest assumption you proved
- The highest‑scoring pain factor
- The clearest gap statement

Those signals should anchor your offer tests. Keep them visible.

## If Two Problems Tie
If you are stuck between two strong problems, use this tie‑breaker:

- Choose the one with a clearer access path to buyers
- Choose the one with faster proof tests
- Choose the one with a stronger gap in the alternatives scan

If they still tie, pick one and commit to a two‑week offer test. The decision
is part of the evidence.

## If You Want to Move Faster
Speed is useful only when the evidence keeps up. Use these safe shortcuts:

- Drop all but the strongest problem and go deeper
- Retest the top risk with a tighter pass line
- Focus the alternatives scan on the top three options buyers mention

Moving fast means narrowing the focus, not skipping the proof.

## After You Leave This Stage
You will feel pressure to jump to the offer. Resist the urge to decorate it
before you test it. Stage 3 is about commitment, not persuasion.

If you skip this discipline, you may spend months polishing a promise that no
one is ready to make. Stage 2 is the cheaper place to be skeptical.

## The Handoff to Stage 3: Earn
Stage 3 will test whether people will commit to your offer and at what price.
That work only makes sense if the Stage 2 diagnosis is honest.

You are about to move from “Is this problem worth solving?” to “Will anyone
commit to a solution?”

## Final Gate
Before you move on, ask: “Would I bet the next three months on this problem?”
If the answer is not a credible yes, tighten the risk, the score, or the gap.

## Closing Line
If you can name the risk, the score, and the gap, you are ready to earn
commitment.
