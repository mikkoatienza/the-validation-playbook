Juicero raised major funding to build a connected juicer that pressed proprietary produce packs. In 2017, reporters showed the packs could be squeezed by hand, and the company shut down later that year. The lesson was clear: the machine solved a problem people did not really have.

That is the rule of this stage. We start where pain already exists, not where a founder wants it to exist. Pain is where payment usually lives. Polish is not proof.

## What "Friction" Means

Friction is the repeated moment where a person says, "This should be easier," and changes their behavior to cope. It shows up as extra steps, extra tools, and extra time. That behavior is signal.

This is not brainstorming. It is detection.

## What "Pay-to-Fix" Pain Means

Pay-to-fix pain is not an irritation. It is a repeated cost in time, money, or risk. Common signs:

- People already spend time or money to reduce it.
- Workarounds are common and visible.
- The cost shows up again and again.

If the pain is real, people usually spend something to reduce it.

## Turn a List Into Patterns

Raw lists are noisy. Patterns are signal. Do a fast pass and group similar frictions together. Give each group a short, plain-language label. Aim for five patterns, not fifty fragments.

1. Highlight repeated words or themes.
2. Group items that feel like the same pain in different clothes.
3. Name the pattern in plain language.
4. Count how often it shows up across sources.

Repetition is a clue. If a pattern appears only once and only in your head, treat it as a hypothesis, not a signal.

## Turn Friction Into Problem Statements

Translate each friction into a clear, testable problem statement.

## The 4-Factor Pain Filter

Not every problem is worth building around. Run each statement through four quick filters and score each 1 to 5.

## The First Decision

Pick your top three problems. These are your candidates. You do not need to commit yet. You only need to test what is riskiest about them.

<Callout type="error" title="Common traps">
- Chasing a "cool idea" with no visible pain
- Using only one source of friction (bias hides the real pattern)
- Writing vague problems that cannot be tested
- Falling in love with your solution before you name the pain
</Callout>
