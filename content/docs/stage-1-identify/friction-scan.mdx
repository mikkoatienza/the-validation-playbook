Juicero raised major funding to build a connected juicer that pressed
proprietary produce packs. In 2017, reporters showed the packs could be
squeezed by hand, and the company shut down later that year. The lesson was
clear: the machine solved a problem people did not really have.

That is the rule of this stage. We start where pain already exists, not
where a founder wants it to exist. Pain is where payment usually lives.
Polish is not proof.

This module is the first step in the Validation Playbook. You will leave
with a list of real problems people will pay to fix and a shortlist worth
testing.

## What "Friction" Means
Friction is the repeated moment where a person says, "This should be easier,"
and changes their behavior to cope. It shows up as extra steps, extra tools,
and extra time. That behavior is signal.

Friction is not the same as a preference. "I wish it looked nicer" is a
preference. "I lose two hours every week doing this by hand" is friction.

This is not brainstorming. It is detection.

## What "Pay-to-Fix" Pain Means
Pay-to-fix pain is not an irritation. It is a repeated cost in time, money,
or risk.

Two quick examples:
- A finance team that loses two days every month reconciling invoices.
- A clinic that misses revenue because appointment no-shows are routine.

Common signs of pay-to-fix pain:
- People already spend time or money to reduce it.
- Workarounds are common and visible.
- The cost shows up again and again.

If the pain is real, people usually spend something to reduce it.

**Output for this module**
- 20 raw frictions
- Top 5 friction patterns
- Top 3 problem candidates

## Where to Look for Friction
You are going to build a list from three sources. Do not filter yet. Capture
everything.

### 1) Personal Friction
Where does your own life or work feel slow or messy?
- A task you repeat every week
- A manual process that drains focus
- A workaround you created because no tool exists
- A step you avoid because it is annoying

### 2) Observed Friction
Where do other people struggle in ways you can see?
- Team processes that break down
- Customers who ask for the same help repeatedly
- Industries with obvious waste or delay
- Handoffs that cause rework or confusion

### 3) Borrowed Friction
Where do people talk about pain in public?
- Forums, communities, review sites
- Support tickets and complaint threads
- "If only this did..." posts
- Pricing complaints and cancellation reasons

## Capture While It Is Fresh
Friction is often easiest to spot in the moment. For one day, keep a quick list of
every annoying step you see. A simple note on your phone is enough. Short
notes beat perfect notes.

## Borrowed Friction Search Prompts
If you need help finding borrowed friction, use simple search phrases in
forums, reviews, and community posts. Look for patterns, not one-offs.

Try:
- "I hate when [task]"
- "[tool] is frustrating"
- "Why does [process] take so long"
- "We switched from [tool] because"
- "Best alternative to [tool]"

## What to Ignore for Now
This stage is not about clever features or trendy ideas. It is about pain
that already exists.

Skip:
- Ideas that require massive behavior change to work.
- Problems that are "annoying" but do not cost anything real.
- Friction that shows up once a year and never again.

You can still write them down, but do not let them dominate your shortlist.

## What a Good Candidate Feels Like
A good candidate is not the flashiest idea. It is the one that keeps showing
up. You can describe it in one breath. You can point to someone who feels it.
You can imagine a test that takes a week, not a quarter.

If you cannot explain the pain in one sentence, you do not know it yet.

**Exercise: 20 Frictions in 20 Minutes**
Set a timer for 20 minutes. Write down 20 points of friction. Do not
judge them. Quantity first.

Rules:
- One friction per line.
- No solutions yet.
- Use the language people actually use.

**Friction capture format**
Write each friction as a single sentence:
"[Person] spends [time/money/effort] on [pain] because [blocker]."

Example (generic):
"Operations managers spend hours each week reconciling shipment delays
because updates live in five different tools."

If nothing feels urgent, go deeper or change the lens, not the goal.

## If You Do Not Have an Idea Yet
Pick one niche you already understand and run the scan there. Proximity
is an advantage. The closer you are to the work, the easier it is to see
what breaks.

Ask:
- What does this group do every week?
- Where do they lose money or time?
- What do they complain about but accept?

Additional prompts:
- What do they avoid because it feels messy or risky?
- What do they do twice because the first pass did not stick?
- What do they outsource because it is too painful in-house?

## Turn a List Into Patterns
Raw lists are noisy. Patterns are signal.

Do a fast pass and group similar frictions together. Give each group a
short, plain-language label. Aim for five patterns, not fifty fragments.

Example pattern labels:
- "Scheduling chaos"
- "Manual reporting"
- "Lost handoffs"

Steps to cluster:
1. Highlight repeated words or themes.
2. Group items that feel like the same pain in different clothes.
3. Name the pattern in plain language.
4. Count how often it shows up across sources.

This helps you see which pain repeats across sources. Repetition is a clue.

**Pattern sanity check**
Promising patterns show up in more than one place. If a pattern appears only
once and only in your head, treat it as a hypothesis, not a signal.

## Turn Friction Into Problem Statements
Translate each friction into a clear, testable problem statement.

**Problem Statement Formula**
"When [trigger] happens, [person] wants to [outcome], but struggles
because [obstacle]."

Example:
"When a freelance designer lands a new client, they want to organize
their week quickly, but struggle because each project has different
timelines and deliverables."

Bad statements are vague. Good statements point to a specific person,
a specific trigger, and a specific obstacle.

Weak vs strong (generic):
- Weak: "People need better onboarding."
- Strong: "When a new hire joins, the ops lead wants a clear checklist,
  but struggles because every team documents it differently."

Quick check:
- Can you picture the person?
- Can you observe the trigger?
- Can you name the obstacle without guessing?

Tightening pass:
- Replace vague words like "better" or "easier" with a real outcome.
- Name the context (team, industry, workflow) in a single phrase.
- Remove anything that sounds like a solution.

**Exercise: Write 10 Problem Statements**
Pick 10 frictions from your list and turn them into problem statements.

Diagram cue: show the problem statement formula + the 4-factor filter as
a simple visual.

## Capture Evidence Notes
For each statement, jot down where it came from and any evidence you saw.
This becomes your raw material for customer interviews in the next module.

Evidence note prompts:
- Source (personal, observed, borrowed)
- How often it shows up
- What people have tried
- Any spend or workaround you can see

## Quantify the Cost (Lightly)
You are not modeling a business yet, but you can estimate the cost of pain.
Ask simple questions:
- How many hours are lost in a typical week?
- What does that time roughly cost in wages or opportunity?
- What breaks when this pain shows up?

Rough numbers are enough. The goal is to see which pains carry weight.

**Quick cost estimate**
- Hours lost per week: ___
- People affected: ___
- Weekly cost (rough): ___

Even a rough estimate helps you compare two problems that feel equally
annoying.

## The 4-Factor Pain Filter
Not every problem is worth building around. Run each statement through
four quick filters:

1. **Frequency**: Does this happen often or rarely?
2. **Intensity**: Is it annoying or truly costly?
3. **Spend**: Are people already spending time or money to fix it?
4. **Workarounds**: Are people hacking solutions together?

Guidance for scoring:
- Frequency: daily/weekly beats quarterly.
- Intensity: revenue loss, risk, or stress beats inconvenience.
- Spend: paid tools, staff time, or outsourced help counts.
- Workarounds: spreadsheets, manual steps, and hacks are evidence.

Score each 1 to 5. The highest scores move forward. Use the scores as a
rough signal, not a verdict.

**Signal vs noise**
If a pain disappears with one quick search, it is usually noise. If people
are still paying, building workarounds, or complaining months later, that is
signal.

When in doubt, look for repetition across sources. A pain you see in your
own work and in public complaints is stronger than a pain you only heard
once. The goal is not certainty. The goal is a short list that is worth the
next test.

## Tie-Breaker (If Scores Are Close)
When two problems score similarly, choose the one where you have:
- Easier access to the people who feel the pain
- More repeatable evidence across sources
- A clearer path to run interviews this week

Speed to evidence beats perfect certainty.

## Common Traps
- Chasing a "cool idea" with no visible pain.
- Using only one source of friction (bias hides the real pattern).
- Writing vague problems that cannot be tested.
- Falling in love with your solution before you name the pain.
- Ignoring workarounds because they look informal.

## The First Decision
Pick your top three problems. These are your candidates. You do not need to
commit yet. You only need to test what is riskiest about them.

Before you move on, do a quick sanity check:
- Can you name the person who feels this pain?
- Can you point to at least one visible workaround?
- Can you explain the cost in one sentence?

If you cannot answer those yet, that is fine. You are early. Capture what
you have and move to the next module, where you will test the pain with
real conversations.

**Closing handoff**
If you cannot find real pain, you do not have an idea yet -- and that is the
point.
