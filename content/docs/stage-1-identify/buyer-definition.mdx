You can have a real pain and a clear solution and still fail if the wrong
person controls the budget. Users can love you, but buyers usually decide.

This module makes the decision path explicit. You will define the Ideal
Customer Profile (ICP), separate user from buyer, and map who triggers,
approves, and pays.

**Output for this module**
- 1 to 2 ICP profiles
- Buyer/user split with decision context
- A clear access path to the buyer

## Buyer vs User (Plain Language)
Users feel the pain. Buyers fund the fix.

Sometimes they are the same person. Often they are not. In B2B, users
press the buttons, but budget holders sign the contract.

If you cannot name the buyer, you are still guessing.

**Quick examples (generic)**
- A support agent needs a faster ticket tool. The buyer is the support
  manager who owns the budget.
- A designer wants a better asset library. The buyer is the head of
  marketing who approves software.
- A teacher wants a better classroom tool. The buyer is the school or
  district that controls purchasing.

Users can be loud. Buyers write checks. Learn the difference early.

## What "ICP" Means
ICP stands for Ideal Customer Profile. It is a simple description of who has
the problem, where it lives, and how urgent it is.

Think role, context, urgency, and access to budget. You are not describing
"everyone." You are describing the person you can reach this month.

**A good ICP includes**
- Role or title
- Environment (team size, industry, workflow)
- Urgency or trigger
- Budget access or influence

**ICP clarity test**
If you cannot picture where they sit and what they touch every week, the
profile is too broad.

## Buyer Signals to Look For
You are not looking for enthusiasm. You are looking for evidence of
decision power.

Signs you have a real buyer:
- They control a budget line.
- They can approve spend without permission.
- They have a metric or key performance indicator (KPI) tied to the pain.
- They have purchased tools like this before.

If none of those are true, you are still talking to a user.

**Budget signals (common)**
- They own a line item on a team budget
- They can expense tools without extra approval
- They have bought similar tools in the last 12 months
- They can approve a pilot or paid trial

If you cannot find any signal, treat the buyer as unknown.
Unknown is better than wrong. This is where many founders lose months.

## A Real Example (Teachmint)
Teachmint started with software for teachers running online classes during
the pandemic. When schools reopened, demand dropped and the teacher-led
model stalled. They shifted toward selling smart classroom hardware and
infrastructure to schools and districts where budgets actually lived. The
users were teachers. The buyers were institutions.

Wrong audience, weak sales, then a buyer shift. That is the lesson.

That shift is what this module is about. The person with the problem is not
always the person who can fund the solution.

## Map the Buyer Path
Most purchases follow a path, even in tiny organizations. Your job is to map
it early.

Ask:
- Who feels the pain first?
- Who approves a fix?
- Who pays?

**Buyer roles (simple map)**
- **Trigger:** notices the pain and asks for help
- **Approver:** can say yes or no
- **Payer:** controls the budget

Sometimes one person fills all three. Often, they do not.

## Buyer Discovery Questions
Use these in your next interviews to clarify who pays.

- "Who signs off on this today?"
- "If you needed a new tool, who would approve it?"
- "Where does the budget come from?"
- "Has your team bought something like this recently?"

If answers are vague, you do not have a buyer yet.

## Decision Context (Why Timing Matters)
Buyers do not decide in a vacuum. They decide inside a budget cycle, a
deadline, or a fire.

Look for:
- A trigger event (new hire, audit, growth spike, regulation change)
- A deadline (quarterly review, launch date, renewal window)
- A metric they own (retention, revenue, compliance, speed)

No context, no urgency. No urgency, no buyer.

## The Champion vs The Buyer
Often the person who feels the pain becomes your champion, not your buyer.
Champions can open doors. Buyers approve spend.

Do not ignore champions. Use them to reach the buyer.

Ask champions:
- "Who else needs to say yes?"
- "What would make this an easy yes for them?"

## Budget Ladder (Simple)
Not all spending works the same way. Map where your offer fits.

- **Personal spend:** the buyer uses a credit card, no approval needed.
- **Team spend:** manager approval, small budget line.
- **Department spend:** procurement or finance involved.

The higher you go, the longer decisions take. Start low when you can.

## Access Path (How You Reach the Buyer)
Knowing the buyer is not enough. You need a path to reach them.

Common access paths:
- Direct outreach (email, LinkedIn)
- Warm intros from users or advisors
- Communities where buyers already gather
- Existing vendor lists inside the company

Pick one path you can test this week. Speed matters more than scale here.

## Budget Proof Tests (Lightweight)
You do not need a full sales process yet, but you do need a signal.

Try:
- Ask for a paid pilot or letter of intent (LOI) (even small)
- Ask what budget line it would come from
- Ask how they bought their last tool

If they cannot answer, you likely have a user, not a buyer.

## Buyer Thesis (One Sentence)
Write one sentence that names the buyer and the outcome they pay for.

Template:
"The buyer is [role] who will pay to [outcome] because [cost of pain]."

Example (generic):
"The buyer is the operations manager who will pay to reduce weekly
reconciliation time because delays cost revenue."

If you cannot write this sentence, your buyer is still unclear.

## Common Traps
- Confusing a loud user with a real buyer.
- Targeting "teams" instead of a specific role.
- Skipping budget signals because the problem feels obvious.
- Selling to the easiest person to reach instead of the person who can pay.
- Hoping virality will fix a missing buyer.

## Red Flags You Picked the Wrong Buyer
- They love the idea but cannot tell you how it would be paid for.
- They need to "check with someone" every time you ask about spend.
- They cannot name any comparable tools their team already buys.

If you hear these, stop guessing. Go find the buyer.

## When Buyer and User Are the Same
In some cases, the buyer and user are the same person. That is fine. Just
prove it. Your interviews should still surface spend, workarounds, and real
actions. Do not assume because it is consumer-facing that the buyer path is
simple.

## When Buyer and User Are Different
This is the default in B2B. Accept it early and design for it.

Users care about ease. Buyers care about outcomes and risk.
Speak to both, but sell to the buyer.

## Buyer/User Split Checklist
Use this to lock the split:
- I can name the primary user in one sentence.
- I can name the budget holder in one sentence.
- I can describe how money moves from buyer to vendor.
- I have at least one real example of prior spend.

## Small Team vs Enterprise (Quick Note)
In small teams, the buyer and user are often the same person. Decisions are
fast. Your job is to find the person who owns the pain and the card.

In larger companies, the path gets longer. You may need a champion, an
approver, and a payer. That is normal. Do not fight it. Map it.

The key is not to win every stakeholder. The key is to reach the one with
authority and a reason to act now.

## The First Decision
Pick one ICP where you have the clearest buyer path and the strongest
budget signal. That is the problem you will diagnose next.

If you are torn between two ICPs, choose the one you can reach fastest.
Speed to evidence beats perfect positioning at this stage.

**Closing line**
If you cannot name the buyer, you are still guessing.
