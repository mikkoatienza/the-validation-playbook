Opinions are easy. Behavior is expensive. That is why your job in this
module is simple: stop collecting compliments and start collecting evidence.

This module takes the top friction patterns from Module 1 and turns them
into proof. You will run interviews that expose what people actually do,
what they have already tried, and what it costs them.

**Output for this module**
- 5 to 8 truth interviews completed
- Evidence notes for each interview (action, spend, workaround)
- A clearer sense of which pain is real

## What a Truth Interview Is
A truth interview is a short, focused conversation designed to surface
past behavior. It is not a sales call, a pitch test, or a product demo.
It is a way to collect evidence about what people already do when the
problem shows up.

The goal is not to be liked. The goal is to be clear.

## Set the Learning Goal
Before you invite anyone, decide what you need to learn.

Write one line:
"I need to learn whether [person] experiences [problem] often enough to
change their behavior or spend to fix it."

This keeps the interview tight. If a question does not serve that goal,
cut it.

## Prep Checklist (5 Minutes)
- Write your problem statement in one sentence.
- List the 3 most important questions you must answer.
- Decide what evidence would count as a "yes."
- Choose where you will take notes.

Clarity before the call saves you from rambling during it.

## The Core Rule
Past behavior beats future promises.

"I would pay for that" is not evidence. "I already paid for something like
that" is. You are not looking for approval. You are looking for proof.

If you leave this module with a stack of opinions, you did not do the work.

Truth interviews can feel uncomfortable at first. That is normal. You are
asking people to recall real moments, not to imagine a future. Stay calm,
stay curious, and let the details surface.

## What Truth Looks Like
Truth is usually visible. It leaves a trail when the pain is real.

Look for:
- **Actions** people already take to reduce the pain
- **Spend** (money, time, or effort) they already invest
- **Workarounds** they built because the problem keeps showing up

If none of those exist, the pain may still be a theory.

### A Real Example (Fanbase to Nextdoor)
Before Nextdoor, the team built a sports platform called Fanbase. Even with
traction, engagement stalled. Instead of building again, they interviewed
neighbors to understand real pain points and tested the idea with crude
prototypes. Those interviews led to the pivot and the eventual launch of
Nextdoor.

That is what truth interviews are for. You are not guessing what people do.
You are watching evidence show up in their story.

Expect to be surprised. If every interview confirms your idea, you are
probably leading the witness. The best interviews correct you early, while
the cost of change is still low.

## Who to Interview
Do not interview random people. Talk to people who live with the problem.

Start with:
- People who match your top friction patterns
- People who have already tried to fix it
- People who feel the pain often enough to remember the last time

Aim for 5 to 8 interviews. That is enough to see a pattern without drowning
in noise.

**Good targets**
- Recent, specific experience ("last week" beats "sometime last year")
- Clear context (role, workflow, environment)
- Evidence of action (they tried something, paid someone, or built a workaround)

**Avoid for now**
- People who only have opinions
- People who have never felt the pain
- People who love your idea but cannot describe a real moment

## How to Invite People (Simple Script)
Keep the invite short, honest, and specific. You are asking for a favor,
not selling a vision.

**Message template**
"Hey [name], I am researching how [role] handles [problem]. I am not
selling anything. Could I ask you a few questions about the last time it
happened? It will take 15 minutes."

If they say yes, book it quickly. If they say no, thank them and move on.

**Where to find people**
- Past colleagues or peers in the same role
- Industry communities or niche Slack groups
- Forum threads where the pain is already visible
- LinkedIn search by role and industry

## Interview Setup
Keep it short. Fifteen to twenty minutes is enough.

Set the tone:
- "I am here to learn, not to pitch."
- "There are no right answers."
- "Real examples help me most."

**Simple timebox (15 minutes)**
- 2 minutes: context and warm-up
- 8 minutes: last time, tried/paid, stakes
- 3 minutes: clarify details and costs
- 2 minutes: thank them and ask for one referral

Surveys are usually too shallow at this stage. You need stories with
sequence and detail. Live conversations give you that context.

## The 4-Step Interview Path
These interviews follow a simple flow. Keep it tight and focused.

**Step 1: Context**
- "Walk me through a normal week."
- "Where does this problem show up?"

**Step 2: Last Time**
- "When was the last time this happened?"
- "What did you do next?"

**Step 3: Tried or Paid**
- "What have you already tried?"
- "Have you paid for anything to solve it?"

**Step 4: Stakes**
- "What did it cost you when it went wrong?"
- "What does a good outcome look like?"

This sequence forces real examples instead of hypothetical answers.

## Question Bank (Use as Needed)
You do not need all of these. Pick the ones that fit the goal.

Behavior questions:
- "What is the very first thing you did?"
- "What did you try next?"
- "Who else was involved?"

Spend questions:
- "Did you pay for a tool or service?"
- "How much time did it take to deal with this?"
- "Who approved the spend?"

Stakes questions:
- "What broke when it failed?"
- "What did it block you from doing?"

## Bad Questions vs Better Questions
Bad questions create polite answers. Better questions create evidence.

- Bad: "Would you use a tool that fixes this?"
  Better: "What did you do the last time this happened?"

- Bad: "Would you pay for this?"
  Better: "Have you paid for anything to solve this?"

- Bad: "Do you like this idea?"
  Better: "What do you hate most about how you do it today?"

## How to Handle Compliments
Some people will say, "This sounds great." Do not chase that. Bring them
back to behavior.

Try:
- "That is helpful. When did you last deal with this?"
- "What have you already tried?"
- "What did you pay, if anything?"

Compliments are not the goal. Evidence is.

## The No-Pitch Rule
Do not sell. Do not hint. Do not teach. Ask and listen.

The moment you pitch, the interview becomes a performance. People start
protecting your feelings and hiding their truth. Your goal is evidence, not
encouragement.

If you need a line, use this:
"I am not selling anything. I am trying to understand how people handle
[problem area]. Can I ask a few questions?"

## The Silence Advantage
After a short answer, pause. People often fill silence with the real detail.
That detail is where the truth lives.

Do not rush it. Let the pause do its job.

**Listening ratio**
Aim for 80% listening, 20% talking. If you are explaining, you are not
learning.

## Using the 5 Whys (Lightly)
When you hear a real example, ask "why" one or two times to reach the root.
Do not interrogate. You are not a prosecutor. You are trying to see the
real cause behind the behavior.

Example:
"Why did you pick that tool?"
Pause. Then: "Why that, instead of the alternative?"

## Synthesize the Evidence
After each interview, write a two-line summary:
1) What happened last time
2) What they did to fix it

Then, after all interviews, look for repetition:
- Same workaround shows up across people
- Same cost shows up across contexts
- Same trigger repeats in different roles

Patterns are stronger evidence. Single stories are signals, not proof.

## Quick Debrief (2 Minutes)
Right after each interview, write two lines:
- "What did they actually do?"
- "What surprised me?"

Small debriefs prevent memory drift and help you see patterns sooner.

## Exercise: Run 5-8 Truth Interviews
Schedule five interviews this week. Keep them short and focused.

**Simple plan**
1. Invite 8 to 12 people to get 5 to 8 interviews.
2. Use the 4-step path. Do not improvise a pitch.
3. Capture evidence notes within 10 minutes of each call.
4. Look for repeatable action and spend.

If you are nervous, start with someone you already know in the space.
Then move outward.

## What Strong Evidence Looks Like
- They describe a recent, specific moment without guessing.
- They name a workaround or tool they already use.
- They mention time, money, or effort spent to cope.

## Red Flags That Pain Is Weak
- They cannot remember the last time it happened.
- They have never tried to fix it.
- The cost feels like a minor annoyance, not a repeated drain.

## What to Do If Evidence Is Mixed
Mixed evidence is normal. Do not force a conclusion too early.

If half the people show real pain and half do not, zoom in:
- Are you talking to the right role?
- Is the context the same across interviews?
- Did the pain show up in the same workflow?

If the pattern is still unclear, run 2 to 3 more interviews with tighter
criteria. Clarity often comes from one more round.

## Common Traps
- Asking hypothetical questions ("Would you use...?").
- Interviewing only friends who want to be nice.
- Talking too much and filling the silence yourself.
- Ignoring spend and workarounds because they feel small.
- Recording notes without concrete examples.

## When You Can Move On
You are ready for Module 3 when you can say:
- "I have heard the same pain in multiple interviews."
- "I can describe what people do to fix it today."
- "I have at least one example of spend or a visible workaround."

If you cannot say those yet, keep interviewing. Do not rush this step.

## The Handoff to Module 3
Once you have evidence, you have to name the buyer with budget and
authority, not just the user with opinions. That is the next step.

**Closing line**
If no one has tried to fix it, it is not pain yet.
