If your offer earns commitment but no one will pay the price, the offer is
still fiction. Price is not a number. It is proof.

This module helps you find a workable price range by testing willingness to
pay in the simplest way possible.

**Output for this module**
- A tested price range
- Buyer reactions and objections
- A clear next price test

## Price Is a Range, Not a Point
Early pricing is not precision. It is a range you can live with.

Think in three bands:
- **Low**: easiest to say yes to
- **Mid**: fair value for the outcome
- **High**: tests true willingness to pay

Your job is to find the band that earns commitment without killing demand.

## Price Anchors (What Buyers Compare To)
Buyers never evaluate price in a vacuum. They compare it to something.

Common anchors:
- What they pay today (tools, staff time, agencies)
- The cost of doing nothing (lost revenue, delays)
- A replacement cost (new hire, consultant, overtime)

If you do not know the anchor, your price will feel random.

## Price vs Value (Quick Frame)
Price is what they pay. Value is what they believe they get.

If value is unclear, price feels high.
If value is obvious, price feels fair.

Your job is to make value obvious in one sentence.

## Anchor Check (Fast)
Before you set your ladder, ask:
- "What does the alternative cost per month?"
- "What does a manual fix cost in hours?"
- "What would a new hire cost to solve this?"

If your price is higher than every anchor, you need a stronger outcome.
If it is lower than every anchor, you may be underpricing the value.

## The Price Ladder Test
Use a simple ladder to test willingness to pay:

1) Low price  
2) Mid price  
3) High price

Pick numbers you can defend. Then see where people say yes.

Diagram cue: a price ladder with pass/fail markers.

## Ladder Example (Generic)
Problem: "Reduce no-shows for small clinics."

- Low: $49/month
- Mid: $149/month
- High: $299/month

The goal is not the number. The goal is which rung gets real yeses.

## How to Choose Your Three Prices
Use this to build your ladder:

- **Low** = "I can say yes quickly"
- **Mid** = "This feels fair for the outcome"
- **High** = "This would be a stretch"

If your ladder is too tight, you learn nothing. If it is too wide, you
break trust. Keep it realistic.

## Price Framing Rules
How you present the price changes the reaction.

Use:
- "This saves you [outcome], so the price is [X]."
- "This replaces [cost], so it pays for itself in [time]."

Avoid:
- Apologizing for the price
- Leading with discounts
- Changing the price mid-conversation

## A Real Example (Netflix Pricing Backlash)
In 2011, Netflix raised prices and split its DVD and streaming plans. The
change triggered backlash and subscriber losses, and the company reversed
course. The lesson was simple: pricing that feels wrong can kill demand
even when the product is loved.

Pricing is not just math. It is perception.

## Choose a Price Metric
Price is not just "how much." It is "how much for what."

Pick one:
- **Per month** (simple and common)
- **Per seat** (if value scales with users)
- **Per outcome** (if results are clear and measurable)
- **Per project** (if work is one-off)

If the metric does not match how value is created, pricing will feel unfair.

## Packaging (Keep It Simple)
Early on, keep packages minimal:
- One plan with a clear outcome
- Two plans if you must (basic and premium)

Too many tiers hide signal. You are not optimizing revenue yet. You are
testing willingness to pay.

## Quick Willingness Range (No Jargon)
Ask two questions to bracket a range:
- "At what price does this feel too expensive to try?"
- "At what price does this feel too cheap to trust?"

The answers give you a band, not a verdict. Use them to set your ladder.

## Price Reaction Signals
Listen to how they respond:
- "That seems fair." (good signal)
- "That's high, but maybe." (test)
- "No way." (price is off or value unclear)

Tone matters as much as words. Capture both.

## What a "Yes" Actually Means
A real yes is a commitment, not a compliment.

Strong signal:
- They accept a price and take the next step

Weak signal:
- "Maybe later"
- "Let me think about it"
- "If you were cheaper, I would"

## Price Split Test (Simple)
If you can run two versions, test two prices with the same offer.

- Version A: mid price
- Version B: high price

If the conversion drops hard, your ceiling is too high. If it holds,
you have room.

## Pricing Questions That Work
Ask about real behavior, not feelings.

Use:
- "Have you paid for anything like this?"
- "What do you pay today to reduce this pain?"
- "If this saved you [outcome], what would be fair?"

Avoid:
- "What would you like to pay?"
- "Would you pay $X?" without context

Context creates truth. Guessing creates noise.

## The Price Floor
Never test below the cost of delivering the result. If you do, you get
false positives and a broken business.

If you cannot name a floor, you are not ready to test price yet.

## The Price Ceiling
The ceiling is the highest price that still gets a "yes" without heavy
negotiation.

If every "yes" requires a discount, your ceiling is too high.

## Exercise: Run a Price Ladder Test
Use one of these formats:

- Add three price options to your offer test
- Ask buyers to choose a plan on a pricing page
- Present three prices in a direct call and ask which feels fair

Then capture:
- Which price they choose
- What made them hesitate
- What they compare it to

## Price Test Menu (By Commitment)
- **Paid waitlist** (low commitment)
- **Deposit or pre-order** (stronger)
- **Paid pilot** (strongest signal)

If you can test a paid pilot, do it. It tells you more than a survey.

## Price Ladder Worksheet (Simple)
Write the ladder and test notes in one place:

| Low | Mid | High | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| | | | |

Add who said yes, who said no, and why. This makes patterns obvious.

## Pre-Sell Script (If You Need One)
Keep it simple and honest:

"I am testing a small offer for [role]. It helps [outcome] without [pain].
If this is valuable, I can run a paid pilot this month. Would you commit
to [price/step] if it worked?"

The script is not the test. The commitment is.

## When to Re-Test
Re-test pricing if:
- You changed the offer outcome
- You changed the buyer
- Your commitment rate dropped

Price only means something in a stable context.

## Price Evidence Notes
For each response, capture:
- Price chosen
- Reason given
- Objection raised
- Comparison tool or budget anchor

If you only record the number, you miss the reason.

## Price Confidence Score (Optional)
Rate your confidence in the price range:

- 1: guess
- 3: mixed evidence
- 5: repeatable commitments

If you are below 3, keep testing.

## Objection Follow-Up Questions
When you hear a price objection, ask one follow-up:

- "What would make this a clear yes?"
- "Is it the price or the outcome that feels off?"
- "Who else would need to approve this?"

These answers tell you whether the price is wrong or the value is unclear.

## Objection Patterns to Track
If you hear these, your price or offer is off:
- "We already pay for something similar."
- "This is a nice-to-have."
- "We do not have budget for this."

Do not argue. Capture the pattern.

## Evidence Artifact
Your evidence artifact is a tested range with notes:
- Low, mid, high price points
- # of buyers at each point
- Common objections

This is the input for your final pricing decision.

## Payment Signals (What Counts)
Stronger signals:
- Paid pilot or deposit
- Signed LOI
- Budget approval in writing

Weaker signals:
- Verbal interest with no next step
- "Let's revisit in Q3"

If all you have is weak signals, you do not have a price yet.

## Price Range Rules
- If only the low price works, your offer is too weak.
- If the high price works with no resistance, your problem is strong.
- If the mid price works, you have a usable starting point.

Cheap does not equal paid. Paid equals paid.

## Monthly vs Annual (Quick Choice)
Annual pricing can create larger commitment, but it is harder to test.

Use:
- Monthly for early tests (easier yes)
- Annual once value is proven (stronger signal)

Do not jump to annual pricing if you cannot earn a monthly yes first.

## If Price Is the Only Objection
If buyers want the outcome but stall on price, try one change:
- Shrink the offer
- Shorten the timeline
- Narrow the segment

Do not rush to discounts. Fix the value first.

## Discount Rule (Use Sparingly)
Discounts are for learning, not for hiding weak value.

If you discount, record:
- Why you did it
- Whether commitment increased
- What that says about the true price

## Common Traps
- Pricing to be liked instead of to be paid
- Hiding the price until later
- Ignoring what buyers already pay
- Calling it "premium" without proof

If nobody pays, the price is fiction.

## When Price and Value Align
You will feel it when price and value click:
- Buyers stop negotiating and start asking how to start
- The price feels fair, not surprising
- You can repeat the commitment with similar buyers

That is the signal to move forward.

## Reality Check (30 Seconds)
Ask yourself:
- "Would I pay this price if I were them?"
- "Do I have evidence, or just confidence?"
- "Is the value obvious in one sentence?"

If the answers are weak, the price is not ready.

## When You Can Move On
You are ready for Module 3 when:
- One price band earns real commitment
- Objections are about timing, not value
- You can explain why people said yes or no

If none of those are true, run another ladder test.

## Price Review (Weekly)
Revisit pricing weekly for the first month:
- What price got real commitment?
- What objections showed up repeatedly?
- What changed in the buyer context?

Pricing is not a one-time decision. It is a learning loop.

## The Bridge to Module 3
Once you have a working price range, the next question is how to reach
buyers efficiently.

**Closing line**
If nobody pays, the price is fiction.
