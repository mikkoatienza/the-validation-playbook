Your offer is the first real proof that your idea deserves to exist. A
feature list can get compliments. An offer asks for commitment. Only one
of those predicts demand.

This module turns your validated problem into a testable offer and asks
for a real yes before you build anything heavy.

**Output for this module**
- An offer thesis
- A commitment test and pass line
- A commitment result with notes

## What an Offer Is (Plain Language)
An offer is a clear promise to a specific person. It is not a list of
features. It is a commitment exchange:

"If you give me [time/money/access], I will give you [outcome] without
[pain]."

If the offer is fuzzy, the commitment will be too.

## Offer vs Feature (Quick Check)
Features describe what the product does. Offers describe what the customer
gets.

- Feature: "Automated reminders"
- Offer: "Reduce no-shows without manual follow-ups"

If you cannot say the outcome in one line, you are not ready to ask for
commitment.

## The Offer Thesis
Use this formula:

**Offer Thesis**
"We help [person] achieve [outcome] without [pain], by [approach]."

Example (generic):
"We help small clinic admins reduce no-shows without manual follow-ups,
by automating deposits and reminders."

If you cannot finish this sentence, you are not ready to test yet.

## Offer Clarity Checklist
Before you test, confirm:
- The person is specific (role + context).
- The outcome is clear and measurable.
- The pain is real and present.
- The approach is believable in one sentence.

If any part is vague, rewrite the offer before you test.

## Offer Language Rules
Keep the offer readable by a first-time founder:
- Use plain language, not buzzwords.
- Lead with the outcome, not the tech.
- Remove words like "platform," "ecosystem," or "AI-powered" unless they
  are required to understand the result.

If a buyer has to decode your sentence, they will not commit to it.

## The Commitment Ladder
Commitment is a spectrum. You are aiming for the highest commitment you
can reasonably ask for right now.

From weakest to strongest:
1. "This sounds interesting."
2. Email signup
3. Calendar booking
4. Paid pilot or deposit
5. Letter of intent

Diagram cue: a simple ladder that climbs from interest to commitment.

## Which Rung Should You Use?
Choose the highest commitment that is realistic for your stage.

- Early and uncertain: calendar booking or deposit
- Strong evidence: paid pilot or LOI
- High urgency + clear pain: pre-order

If you are always stuck on email signups, you are avoiding the truth.

## Commitment Types (Three Signals)
Commitment shows up in three forms:

- **Time**: calendar booking, pilot participation
- **Money**: deposit, pre-order, paid trial
- **Access**: data, workflow access, internal approval

Time is a signal. Money is a stronger signal. Access is often the strongest
signal in B2B because it is hard to fake.

If you can get access, you are closer to truth than a hundred compliments.

## A Real Example (Buffer)
Buffer did not start with a full product. Founder Joel Gascoigne built a
simple landing page and added a pricing step before signup. If people chose
a paid plan, that was commitment. If they only left an email, that was
interest. The pricing step exposed the gap between real intent and polite
interest, so he did not mistake clicks for demand.

The pricing step separated real intent from polite interest. That is the
difference between a yes and a maybe.

What matters is the structure: ask for commitment before you build, and
let behavior decide whether the idea deserves the next step.

## What Fake Interest Looks Like
These are easy to get and easy to misread:
- "Cool idea, keep me posted."
- "I would use it" with no next step.
- "Maybe later" with no urgency.

If that is all you get, the offer is still a guess.

## Exercise: Write Your Offer and Choose a Test
You need two things: the offer and the test.

**Step 1: Write the offer**
- Who is it for?
- What outcome are they buying?
- What pain does it remove?

**Step 2: Choose one commitment test**
Pick the smallest test that still asks for a real yes.

Options:
- Paid waitlist
- Paid pilot
- Pre-order
- Calendar booking with a clear price
- Letter of intent

The goal is not to scale. The goal is to prove demand.

## Offer Test Menu (By Rung)
Choose the smallest test that still asks for a real yes:

- **Email signup** (weak): only if the market is totally unknown
- **Calendar booking** (medium): shows time commitment
- **Deposit** (strong): shows willingness to pay
- **Paid pilot** (stronger): shows budget + urgency
- **LOI** (strongest): shows executive intent

If you can do a paid pilot, skip the email list.

## Who to Test With
Your offer test is only as good as your audience.

Test with:
- People who feel the pain today
- People who can decide or influence spend
- People who match your ICP from Stage 1

Avoid:
- Friends who want to be supportive
- People outside the problem context
- Curious observers who will never buy

## Offer Test Card
Write this before you run any test:
- We believe [buyer] will commit to [offer].
- To test this, we will [smallest action].
- We will measure [signal].
- We are right if [pass line].
- Next step if right: [move forward].
- Next step if wrong: [change/stop].

One test card per offer keeps you focused.

**Test card example (generic)**
- We believe clinic admins will pay $150/month to reduce no-shows.
- To test this, we will offer a paid pilot to 10 clinics.
- We will measure pilot commitments.
- We are right if 3 clinics pay within 14 days.
- Next step if right: expand to 10 more clinics.
- Next step if wrong: change the offer or the buyer.

## Simple 7-Day Offer Test
Day 1: Write the offer and pass line  
Day 2: Build the test page or pitch  
Day 3: Reach 10 to 20 qualified people  
Day 4-5: Run the test and track responses  
Day 6: Review commitment results  
Day 7: Decide the next test

Speed beats perfection. You are buying evidence, not building a brand.

## Define the Pass Line
Before you run the test, define what "pass" looks like.

Examples:
- "3 out of 15 people commit to a paid pilot in two weeks."
- "5 qualified buyers book and show up to a paid discovery call."
- "2 teams pay a deposit within 10 days."

No pass line, no decision. A test without a pass line is just activity.

## Pass Line Guardrails
- Make the pass line hard enough to matter.
- Make it small enough to hit in two weeks.
- Tie it to commitment, not clicks.

If you can move the line after the test, it was never a pass line.

## Pass Line Math (Simple)
Your pass line should feel slightly uncomfortable, not impossible.

Use a simple ratio:
- 20% commitment for a warm list
- 10% commitment for a cold list

If your list is tiny, focus on absolute numbers (e.g., 3 paid pilots).

## The Minimum Viable Offer
Your first offer should feel small enough to say yes to quickly.

Ask:
- Can they try it without a long contract?
- Can they decide in under a week?
- Is the outcome clear enough to judge?

If the offer feels heavy, shrink it until it is testable.

## How to Shrink the Offer
Reduce scope without losing the outcome:
- Shorten the time window (7 days instead of 90)
- Narrow the segment (one role, one workflow)
- Limit the deliverable (one report, one setup, one outcome)

Small offers create fast answers. Fast answers beat perfect offers.

## Offer Test Page (Simple Structure)
If you are using a landing page, keep it short and focused.

1) Headline with the outcome  
2) One short paragraph on the pain  
3) The offer in one sentence  
4) The commitment ask (price or next step)

If you need three pages to explain it, the offer is not clear.

## No Landing Page? Use Direct Outreach
You can test an offer without a site.

Options:
- A one-page PDF with the offer and price
- A short email with a calendar link
- A simple message with a clear ask

The format does not matter. The commitment does.

## Offer Page Checklist
- Outcome is in the headline
- Pain is in the first paragraph
- Commitment ask is visible without scrolling
- Price is shown if you can show it

If any of these are missing, you will collect interest, not commitment.

## Pre-Sell Script (If You Need One)
Keep it simple and honest:

"I am testing a small offer for [role]. It helps [outcome] without [pain].
If this is valuable, I can run a paid pilot this month. Would you commit
to [price/step] if it worked?"

The script is not the test. The commitment is.

## Evidence Artifact
Capture the results with notes:
- Who committed
- What they agreed to
- Why they said yes
- What objections showed up

This is your proof that the offer earns commitment or it does not.

## Commitment Result Summary
Write two lines:
- "We asked for [commitment]. We got [result]."
- "This means [offer is strong / offer is weak]."

Short summaries keep you honest.

## When Commitment Is Strong
Strong commitment means:
- The buyer agrees quickly without heavy persuasion
- The objection is about timing, not value
- You can repeat the result with another buyer

If it is not repeatable, it is not proof yet.

## Quick Follow-Up (After a Yes)
Do not disappear after a commitment. Capture what made it easy.

Ask:
- "What made this a clear yes?"
- "What would have made you say no?"

These answers sharpen the offer before you scale it.

## Objection Pattern Log
If you hear the same objection twice, write it down. Common objections
tell you what to fix:
- "Too early"
- "Not urgent"
- "We already have something"
- "Budget is frozen"

Do not argue. Treat objections as data.

## If Commitment Is Weak
Weak commitment usually means one of three things:
1) The problem is not painful enough.
2) The buyer is wrong.
3) The offer is unclear or too big.

Do not add features. Tighten the offer or change the buyer.

## Common Traps
- Soft calls to action (CTAs) that only collect compliments
- Vague outcomes ("better", "easier", "faster")
- Testing the offer with fans instead of buyers
- Hiding the price to avoid rejection

Compliments are noise. Commitments are proof.

## When You Can Move On
You are ready for Module 2 when:
- You have a clear commitment signal
- You can explain why people said yes or no
- You have a pass line result to act on

If you cannot name those, run the test again or change the offer.

## The Bridge to Module 2
If you get commitment, the next question is price. If you do not, the offer
is still fiction.

**Closing line**
No commitment, no offer.
