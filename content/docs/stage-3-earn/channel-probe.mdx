A great offer with no channel is still a dead idea. You do not need a
marketing plan yet. You need proof that you can reach real buyers without
burning a year.

This module helps you test 2 to 3 channels quickly and decide which path
can deliver predictable access to your ICP.

**Output for this module**
- A tested channel hypothesis (2 to 3 channels)
- Early signal from at least one channel
- Notes on cost, speed, and buyer fit

## What a Channel Is (Plain Language)
A channel is where your buyers already are and already paying attention.
It is not a tactic. It is a path to access.

An audience is not the same thing. An audience might watch. A channel lets
you reach a buyer and ask for a next step.

Examples:
- A niche community where your buyer asks questions
- A marketplace where they already shop
- A partner who already sells to them

If you cannot reach buyers, nothing else matters.

## The 3-Channel Rule
Pick only two or three channels to test. More than that is noise.

You are not scaling. You are probing. Small tests beat big plans.

Why three? Because you can actually run the tests in one week and compare
signal without drowning in data. Two or three is enough to learn.

## Channel Test Matrix
Score each channel on three dimensions:

- **Reach**: how many ICPs you can access
- **Cost**: money + time to get a response
- **Speed**: how fast you can get a signal

Diagram cue: a simple 3-column matrix (Reach / Cost / Speed).

**Quick scoring guide**
- Reach 1 = you can name 5 people, Reach 5 = you can name 50
- Cost 1 = expensive or slow, Cost 5 = cheap and fast
- Speed 1 = weeks, Speed 5 = days

**Tie-breaker rules**
- If two channels look equal, pick the faster one
- If speed is equal, pick the cheaper one
- If cost is equal, pick the one closest to the buyer

## Exercise: Pick 3 Channels
Use this short toolkit, then make your picks.

You are not searching for the perfect channel. You are choosing the fastest
path to a real buyer conversation.

**Channel categories (quick map)**
- **Outbound**: direct email, LinkedIn, cold calls
- **Communities**: Slack groups, forums, subreddits
- **Partnerships**: agencies, consultants, platforms
- **Marketplaces**: where buyers already purchase tools
- **Content/SEO**: high intent searches

Pick categories you can actually touch this week. If you cannot reach the
buyer in seven days, it is not a real test.

**Channel vs tactic**
- Channel: LinkedIn  
- Tactic: 20 personalized DMs

- Channel: a partner newsletter  
- Tactic: one co-written, targeted email to their list

If you change tactics without changing the channel, you do not really learn.

**Channel-to-offer match**
- Outbound: short, specific offer with a fast next step
- Community: insight + offer, not a pitch
- Partner: shared outcome and shared upside

If your next step is too big, the channel will look weak. Keep the ask small.
Early asks that tend to work:
- A 15-minute discovery call
- A short problem walkthrough
- A yes/no commitment to review a one-page offer

**Channel message match**
- Community: ask a question and share a short insight
- Outbound: one sentence on the pain + a clear ask
- Partner: explain the outcome and the mutual benefit

Example (outbound):
"We help clinic admins cut no-shows by 20%. Open to a 15-minute call?"

**Message length rule**
One screen, one ask. If it needs a paragraph to explain, your offer is still
fuzzy.

**Channel signals (what counts)**
- A booked call
- A reply that confirms the pain
- A referral to the buyer
- A paid pilot or deposit

Signals are actions, not compliments. Interest without action is only a clue.
Weak signals to ignore for now:
- Likes, views, or impressions
- "Sounds cool" with no next step
- Feedback from people who are not your buyer

You are not measuring awareness. You are measuring willingness to act.

**Channel fit questions**
- Are buyers already active here?
- Can I reach them repeatedly?
- Does this channel get better with repetition?
- Can I test this without a long setup?

If any answer is no, lower the channel score and move on.

**Channel bet template**
- Channel:
- Why buyers are there:
- Test you will run:
- Pass signal:
- Where I will get the first 10 names:

Write it fast. Clarity beats polish at this stage.

**Example channel bet (generic)**
- Channel: regional clinic admin groups
- Why buyers are there: they swap compliance checklists
- Test: 15 targeted messages asking for a short call
- Pass signal: 3 booked calls in one week

**Pass signal examples**
- 5 qualified replies from 20 messages
- 3 booked calls from a community post
- 2 warm intros from one partner
- A buyer forwards you to the decision maker

If the signal is only views or likes, it does not count.
Turn attention into a conversation.

**Qualified response**
Count a response only if it meets both:
- The person matches your ICP or can reach them
- They describe the pain in their own words or accept a clear next step

## Exercise: Run a Tiny Channel Test
For each channel, run a small test in 7 days.

Examples:
- 20 direct messages to ICPs
- 2 community posts + 10 replies
- 1 partner intro + 3 warm conversations

You are not trying to win. You are trying to learn.
One clean test beats three messy ones. Define one ask and repeat it.

## 7-Day Channel Sprint
Day 1: Choose 2-3 channels and define pass signals  
Day 2: Build a short message or post  
Day 3: Run the test (send, post, or ask for intros)  
Day 4-5: Follow up and track responses  
Day 6: Review signals and costs  
Day 7: Pick the channel to focus next

Speed beats depth at this stage.
Follow-up is part of the test. Many real responses show up after the reminder.

**Response benchmarks (early)**
- 10% reply rate on cold outreach is a useful signal
- 20% response rate from a community post is strong
- 1 to 2 warm intros from a partner is enough to keep testing

If you are unsure, treat one real buyer conversation as a pass for week one.
Consistency matters more than volume in the first test.

**Channel retest rule**
If the channel only works once, it does not work. Run the same test twice.
If the second test fails, lower your confidence score.

**Channel kill criteria**
- You cannot reach buyers after two tests
- Responses are consistently unqualified
- The cost per conversation is unsustainable
- The channel requires months of setup before a signal

If you keep it anyway, be honest about the trade-off. You are choosing a
higher-risk bet.

**If a channel works**
- Double down for two weeks
- Focus on one message, one segment, one offer
- Increase volume only after you repeat the result

Do not add a new channel during this window. The goal is repeatable access,
not novelty.

**If no channel works**
- Tighten the ICP
- Narrow the problem
- Try a different buyer segment
- Re-check your buyer definition from Stage 1

If you are reaching the wrong person, every channel looks broken.

## Evidence Artifact
Capture channel results in one place:
- Channel tested
- # of attempts
- # of qualified responses
- # of buyer conversations
- Notes on cost and speed
- The exact message or post that produced signal

If the channel does not reach buyers, it does not count.

This artifact is your channel memory. Without it, you repeat the same mistakes.

**Cost per conversation (quick math)**
Add money spent and hours spent, then divide by qualified responses.
You do not need precision. You need a gut check.

**Channel log (simple)**
| Channel | Attempts | Qualified responses | Calls | Cost | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| | | | | | |

In the notes, capture the exact phrasing that got a reply and the main
objection that stopped a call. Those two lines guide your next test.

**Channel wedge (one sentence)**
"We reach [ICP] through [channel] because [reason]."

Example (generic):
"We reach clinic admins through regional associations because they trade
compliance checklists there."

## A Real Example (Airbnb and Craigslist)
Airbnb first tried to grow by attracting hosts directly, but early traction was
slow. The team then tapped Craigslist, where people were already listing
short-term rentals. By building a way to cross-post listings, they reached
hosts where they already were. That correction unlocked early growth.

The product did not change. The channel did.
Distribution turned a weak signal into a real one.

## Common Traps
- Chasing every channel at once
- Counting clicks instead of conversations
- Mistaking attention for buyer access
- Choosing a channel because it feels familiar
- Changing your message mid-test
- Talking to users when the buyer is someone else
- Assuming volume will fix a weak message

If you cannot reach buyers, your offer will die in silence.

**Closing line**
No channel, no customers.
