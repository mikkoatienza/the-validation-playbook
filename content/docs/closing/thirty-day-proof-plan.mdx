You have the playbook. What you need now is a short, disciplined sprint that
turns the ideas into evidence. This closing chapter is a 30‑day proof plan you
can run immediately. It is not a motivational wrap‑up. It is a decision path.

Adjust the pace if you need to, but keep the sequence. The order is the logic.

## How the 30‑Day Plan Works
Each week maps to one core stage of IDEAL. The goal is not to do everything.
The goal is to prove the next step and decide.

You will use three rules:
- **One focus per week.** If you split attention, you split evidence.
- **One pass line.** If you don’t set a line, you don’t decide.
- **One decision.** Every week ends with a clear next move.

Use your Positioning Snapshot and Loop Map from the Extensions as inputs to
Weeks 1–2 decisions. They keep the proof connected to your edge and momentum.

## Minimum Weekly Rhythm
Keep a small, repeatable cadence so the month stays realistic:

- One session to collect evidence
- One session to run the test
- One session to synthesize results
- One short session to decide and log

If that feels too heavy, reduce the scope of the test, not the discipline of
the decision.

Your calendar is part of the evidence. If you consistently cannot make time
for the tests, that signal matters too: the problem may not be urgent enough
for you, or for the buyer you chose.

## The 30‑Day Cadence (At a Glance)
Use this as a quick map of the month:

| Week | Stage | Focus | Decision |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | Identify | Find real friction | Choose one problem |
| 2 | Diagnose | Prove risk + pain | Keep or narrow |
| 3 | Earn | Test commitment + price | Fix or proceed |
| 4 | Assess & Lock | Test repeat use + decide | Commit or stop |

Keep this table visible. It reminds you that every week ends with a decision.

## Week 1 — Identify: Find Real Friction
**Goal:** build a clean problem shortlist based on real pain.

**Why this week matters**
If the pain is weak, every later test is wasted. This week protects you from
building for a problem that only lives in your head.

**Inputs**
- Observed or lived friction
- Your own notes and daily annoyances
- Borrowed friction (forums, reviews, peers)

**Actions**
- Build a friction inventory (20 items).
- Convert friction into 10 clear problem statements.
- Select your top 3 problems.

**Evidence standard (what counts)**
- The pain repeats across sources.
- People already try to fix it.
- The cost is visible (time, money, or risk).

**Output**
- Friction Inventory + top 3 problem candidates.

**Decision at the end of Week 1**
Choose one problem to take into Week 2. If you cannot choose, your list is too
wide—narrow the lens and repeat the scan.

**Pass line**
At least two of your top three problems show repeated pain across sources and
a visible workaround.

## Week 2 — Diagnose: Map the Risk and Score the Pain
**Goal:** prove the problem is worth solving and not already owned.

**Why this week matters**
You are about to commit time, money, and energy. This week makes sure the
problem is urgent and the gap is real.

**Inputs**
- The top problem from Week 1
- Early interview notes (if any)

**Actions**
- Build a Risk Map (list 8–12 assumptions, rank the top risk).
- Score the problem using the Problem Scorecard.
- Scan alternatives (3 direct, 3 substitutes, 3 DIY workarounds).

**Evidence standard (what counts)**
- The top assumption is testable this month.
- The problem scores high with real evidence.
- Alternatives leave a visible gap or underserved segment.

**Output**
- Risk Map + Problem Scorecard + Alternatives Grid.

**Decision at the end of Week 2**
If the score is weak or the gap is unclear, narrow the problem and retest.
If the evidence is strong, move forward.

**Pass line**
The top risk is testable within two weeks, the problem score is high, and the
alternatives grid shows a clear gap or underserved segment.

## Week 3 — Earn: Test Commitment and Price
**Goal:** earn real commitment before you build.

**Why this week matters**
Interest is cheap. Commitment is costly. This week tells you if people will
actually pay or invest time.

**Inputs**
- A prioritized problem and clear gap
- A basic offer thesis

**Actions**
- Write one offer thesis for a specific buyer.
- Run an offer test (time or money commitment).
- Test a simple price ladder (low, mid, high).

**Evidence standard (what counts)**
- 3+ real commitments or deposits.
- Clear price reactions (not just compliments).
- Objections you can map and address.

**Output**
- Commitment Log + Price Range Notes.

**Decision at the end of Week 3**
If the offer does not earn commitment, tighten the promise and retest. If the
price fails, reset the ladder. Avoid building yet.

**Pass line**
3+ real commitments or deposits and clear price reactions you can explain.

## Week 4 — Assess & Lock: Prove Repeat Use and Decide
**Goal:** test repeat use and make the commitment decision.

**Why this week matters**
Commitment without repeat use is a fragile signal. This week tells you if
you can earn habit, not just interest.

**Inputs**
- Commitment signal from Week 3
- A minimum test you can run in seven days

**Actions**
- Run the smallest MVP test with a pass line.
- Score PMF signals (repeat use, time‑to‑value, completion).
- Decide: improve, pivot, or stop.
- Write your decision gate statement.

**Week 4 split (keep it tight)**
Days 22–26 are Assess: run the smallest test, watch repeat use, and capture the
loop. Days 27–30 are Lock: run the decision gate, check founder fit, and install
the weekly system that keeps you honest.

**Evidence standard (what counts)**
- A clear pass/fail result on the MVP test.
- Repeat use without heavy prompting.
- A loop you can explain in one sentence.

**Output**
- MVP Test Summary + PMF Signal Score + Decision Note.

**Decision at the end of Week 4**
Commit if the loop is real and the fit is strong. Pause if the loop is weak.
Stop if the evidence is clear and the cost is too high.

**Pass line**
The MVP test hits the pass line, repeat use appears within the expected cycle,
and the loop statement is clear and believable.

## If You Finish Early (Acceleration Path)
If you hit the pass lines early, do not skip weeks. Use the extra time to:

- Retest the strongest signal with a tighter pass line
- Expand to one adjacent segment and compare results
- Strengthen the evidence log and decision note

Fast decisions are good. Repeated decisions are better.

## The Outcome (What You Earn)
At the end of 30 days, you will have one of two results:
- Proof that the idea is worth building, or
- Evidence that it is not, which saves you time and unlocks the next idea.

Both outcomes are wins.

If you are unsure which result you have, the evidence is still thin. Tighten
the test, don’t stretch the timeline.

## If a Week Fails (Recovery Path)
Failure is not a setback if you treat it as evidence. Use this recovery path:

- **Week 1 fails:** Narrow the pain and repeat the scan.
- **Week 2 fails:** Rewrite the top assumption and rescore the problem.
- **Week 3 fails:** Tighten the offer and retest commitment.
- **Week 4 fails:** Fix the loop trigger or change one variable and rerun.

Try not to skip the failed week. Tighten it, then move forward.

## Evidence Standards (What Counts)
Use these quick checks to separate signal from noise:

- **Signal:** buyers commit time or money, return without prompts, or pay again.
- **Noise:** compliments, likes, “maybe,” or interest without action.

If you cannot point to signal, consider not advancing the week.

## What Good Evidence Looks Like (Quick Guide)
Use this to sense‑check your signals:

- **Week 1:** at least two real examples, not hypothetical pain
- **Week 2:** a testable top risk and a clear alternative gap
- **Week 3:** commitments you can count, not compliments you can quote
- **Week 4:** repeat use within a cycle and a loop you can explain

## Decision Rules (Keep It Honest)
At the end of each week, choose one of three outcomes:

- **Advance** if the pass line is met.
- **Tighten** if the signal is mixed or unclear.
- **Reset** if the signal is weak or missing.

Write the decision in one sentence. If you cannot, you may be avoiding it.

## The 30‑Day Proof Summary (Template)
At the end of 30 days, write a one‑page proof summary:

- Problem chosen and why it matters
- Evidence that the pain is real
- Evidence that buyers will commit
- Price range that holds
- Channel that reaches buyers
- Loop statement (why they return)
- Decision: go / pivot / stop

If you cannot fill this summary, you may not be done yet.

## The Proof Board (One Page)
Create a single page you can glance at weekly. It should include:

- The problem you chose
- The top risk and its current status
- The current pass line
- The last decision
- The next test

If your board is empty, your sprint is likely empty.

## The “No‑Excuses” Rule
If a week slips, avoid extending the month unless a test is still in motion.
Tighten the test and move forward. The discipline is the proof.

## The 30‑Day Proof Summary (Filled Example)
This example shows the level of detail you should aim for:

- Problem chosen and why it matters: “Clinic admins lose 4–6 hours per week to no‑show churn.”
- Evidence that the pain is real: “7 of 9 interviews described manual follow‑ups.”
- Evidence that buyers will commit: “4 deposits at $200 for a pilot.”
- Price range that holds: “$150–$300 tested; $250 accepted by 3 buyers.”
- Channel that reaches buyers: “Regional associations produced 6 qualified replies.”
- Loop statement: “They return because weekly reminders reduce revenue loss.”
- Decision: go / pivot / stop: “Go for 30 days with repeat‑use focus.”

If your summary feels vague, the evidence is still weak. Tighten it before you
decide.

## The Proof Metrics You Track
You do not need a dashboard. You do need a few signals:

- # of interviews completed
- # of commitments earned
- Price range acceptance rate
- Repeat‑use rate (within expected cycle)

If those numbers are zero, you are not moving. Change the test.

## The Week‑by‑Week Decision Log
At the end of each week, write a one‑line decision:

- Week 1: “We chose ___ because ___.”
- Week 2: “We narrowed to ___ because ___.”
- Week 3: “We earned ___ because ___.”
- Week 4: “We decided ___ because ___.”

If you cannot write the line, you likely did not decide.

## If You Want to Move Faster
Speed is useful only when the evidence keeps up. Use these safe shortcuts:

- Narrow to one buyer segment and go deeper
- Retest the weakest assumption with a smaller test
- Drop the weakest channel and focus on the strongest

Moving fast means doing less, not skipping proof.

## The 30‑Day Decision Snapshot
At the end of the month, write one final paragraph:

"We tested ___, learned ___, and decided ___ because ___."

This snapshot becomes your handoff to future teammates, advisors, or
investors. It proves you made a decision with evidence.

Use the Proof Board and Evidence Log weekly, the Proof Summary on Day 30, and
the Decision Snapshot as your handoff note.

## Your Weekly Commitment Ritual
Before each week starts, write:

- The riskiest assumption
- The smallest test
- The pass line
- The decision you will make

Do not start the week without a decision baked in. Evidence without decision
is just motion.

Keep a simple log so you do not lose the thread:

## Final Proof Check
Write one sentence: “I would bet the next 90 days on this because ___."
If you cannot write it, slow down and tighten the evidence.

## Final Reminder
Passion is powerful when it is earned. Proof is how you earn it.

## Final Question
What will you test this week to make the next decision inevitable?

## If You Go Beyond 30 Days
If you decide to continue, keep the weekly cadence. That is the real habit.
If you decide to stop, keep the evidence. It will save you on the next idea.

## Final Line
Proof beats promise.
